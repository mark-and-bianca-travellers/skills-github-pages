title: "Swimming pigs, swarming Iguana"  
date:  2026-03-08 12:59:14 -0400

So, it turns out that the English woman serving breakfast in the Sand Piper Café is part of the family that owns Exuma Watersports.  And perhaps, the café too, and the store next door.  Anyhow, the Watersports’ part of the enterprise operates excursions round and about the islands, including a boat trip to a mythical beach where the cutest pigs swim in the sea.  Really.  
  
![glorious white sands and blue sky]({{"/assets/images/blue_sky_beach.png"|relative_url}})  
  
It turns out that this rare sight demands a not-inconsiderable fee; however, what are you going to do? Not swim with pigs?  We booked for the afternoon tour, prepared for the sun, sand and sea, and then walked over to the agreed meeting place where the air-conditioned minibus awaited.  After two on-route stops, our bus-load of Americans, Brits, Brazilians, Canadians and Indians arrived at the top of our island to transfer to a waiting motorboat.  With the addition of a few more people of still further different nationalities, we set off across the bluest of seas, to a line of tiny islands under a deep blue sky.  The ride was thrilling: the bows lifted, driven by two huge outboards, the oncoming wind practically pinning us to our seats. Within minutes we slowed again as we arrived at the home of the aquatic pigs.  
  
![pigs in the sea, chickens on the beach]({{"/assets/images/pigs_chickens.png"|relative_url}})  
  
It was as if the pigs knew that an arriving boat meant carrots in a bucket.  And that a stingray also knew that a gathering of pigs in the sea meant carrots on the ocean floor.  The boat reversed into the beach and an anchor was thrown into the water, before our excursion guide ventured off the back of the boat with the aforementioned bucket.  The beach emptied as the pigs raced into the sea, oddly to be replaced by a similar number of chickens 🤷.  The ship’s complement climbed down the short ladders into the water, taking carrots from the bucket and offering them, with a little squealing from both parties, to the obviously ravenous pigs.  
  
![Mark surrounded by pigs in the sea]({{"/assets/images/pigs_and_mark.png"|relative_url}})  
  
Meanwhile, a ray, perhaps a metre across, brushed against our legs as it hoovered-up anything dropped from the activities happening above.  It was soon at the surface and being fed directly by some of the more intrepid visitors.  “You have to get your hand out of the way, before the teeth get you” was the only instruction given!  
  
![feeding a stingray]({{"/assets/images/ray.png"|relative_url}})  
  
Eventually, after the pigs had had their fill and we’d coaxed them back to the beach, we were all back on the boat, racing off to another encounter with dozens of unlikely creatures on another idyllic beach, this time Iguana.  
  
![offering lettuce to an Iguana]({{"/assets/images/lettuce_and_iguana.png"|relative_url}})  
  
I’ve never been close to an Iguana before; aside from the odd zoo encounter, I may not have seen one before.  So it was doubly strange to have Iguana the size of cats standing on my feet as I fed them lettuce leaves. It occurred to me that my shorts were the colour of lettuce leaves, which did give me a moment of passing concern.  Fortunately, these Iguana didn’t seem able to jump!  Next, to be posing for a photo with an Iguana in my hands was stranger still. I’m not sure how the Iguana felt about it, but it didn’t seem to be too bothered!  
  
![Mark and Bianca with an Iguana]({{"/assets/images/with_iguana.png"|relative_url}})  
  
Back to the boat for drinks and conversation, zipping between other island to walk on white sands, swim in clear water and take-in breathtaking views, before eventually returning to our originating harbour and awaiting minibus, for the journey back to Georgetown.
